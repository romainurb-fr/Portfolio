Model Order Reduction of Composite Wings

Download PDF Report

This project was carried out over a period of four and a half months as part of a research internship at Professor Abe's multi-physics laboratory at Tohoku University in Japan.

Context Problem Methods Results Conclusion

Context

Aviation is a highly polluting sector, and reducing its environmental impact has become a priority. Engineers increasingly rely on numerical simulations to design more efficient and sustainable aircraft, but these simulations generate enormous volumes of data. Composite materials, such as CFRP, though promising, remain complex to model and certify. To overcome these challenges, data-driven approaches such as dimensionality reduction and aerodynamic mode identification help reduce computational costs while maintaining accuracy.

Problem

How can we reduce the order of models used to simulate composite wings while preserving the accuracy of the results?

Methods

To address this challenge, I used a two-step approach:

  • Use of data-driven methods for dimensionality reduction: identification of dominant behaviors in the data using POD (Proper Orthogonal Decomposition) and SOM (Self-Organizing Map) to create relevant and exploitable reduced models.
  • Application of these reduced models to aerodynamic simulations to evaluate their performance and accuracy in reconstructing aeroelastic behaviors.

Results

  • Two reduced models were implemented:
    • One based on reconstruction by POD (Proper Orthogonal Decomposition).
    • One highlighted by SOM (Self-Organizing Map), reconstructed solely by varying the length of the wing.
  • The results show that these models effectively capture the aeroelastic behaviors of composite wings while significantly reducing computation time. Some key results include pressure distribution and Mach number across the wing, as well as the variation of the pressure coefficient on sections of the wing for the original and POD-reconstructed geometries.
  • These approaches offer a viable alternative to traditional methods, opening the door to faster and less costly simulations.

What I Learned

  • This internship gave me the opportunity to deepen my knowledge in aeroelastic simulations, particularly on fluid-structure interaction using the DASH software developed by the lab.
  • I also enhanced my programming skills, especially in Python for post-simulation result processing, and in Linux for managing and running simulations under DASH.
